Journey through the ecosystem

Learning Situation

CONTEXT

Far in the future, the situation on Earth is dire. Several ecosystems have been wrecked because of human interference and species of animals and plants are dwindling in number. However a group of courageous adventurers, called the Guardians of Biodiversity, has been entrusted the task of restoring the planets ecosystems and ensuring the balance of biodiveersity is maintained. 

The further tasks will involve travelling to different ecosystems on the planet using a special passport conceived by the Guardians. A specific stamp will these participants receive after successfully completing a task, based on solving a problem, which is linked to biodiversity loss and the factors threatening the ecosystems. If there is success in completing all the tasks, history will be made as it will be the first time in the world that society has been granted a key known to unlock a natural object referred to as the Box of Nature.

SDGs

  • SDGs 6: Clean water and sanitation: the different challenges show the importance of water by creating awareness among the students and the possible damages they can generate if they don't stop polluting habits. 
  • SDGs 11: sustainable cities and communities: students come up and create solutions in order to make sustainable communities the best as possible. 
  • SDGs 12: responsible consumption and production: students reflect on responsible habits such as recycling and the reduction of residual waste. 
  • SDGs 14: life below water: overall, students will have a glimpse of the marine ecosystems and their challenges. They will also see the contamination due to plastics, biodiversity lost, and how to preserve marine life. 
  • SDGs 15: life on land: overall, students will see the importance of biodiversity and the balance between biotic and abiotic factors, promoting restoration and protection of terrestrial ecosystems. 

Specific competences

  • Mathematical Competence and Competences in Science, Technology, and Engineering (MCTSTE): Problem-solving and reasoning skills in mathematics, science, and technology, fostering scientific thinking and decision-making.
  • Digital Competence (DC): Proficiency in the use of digital tools for information management, communication, and problem-solving ethically and safely.
  • Personal, Social, and Learning to Learn Competence (PSLLC): Self-awareness, collaboration, and learning-to-learn skills to navigate personal and social challenges.
  • Cultural Awareness and Expression Competence (CAEC): Appreciation of cultural expressions and fostering creativity in artistic and cultural contexts.

  • Collaborate effectively, and employ tools and technologies to present solutions to environmental and scientific challenges.

1. How Biotic and Abiotic Components Interrelate in Ecosystems

Biotic components: refer to the living parts of an ecosystem, encompassing plants, animals, microorganisms, fungi, and various organisms. In contrast, abiotic components consist of the non-living factors affecting ecosystems, including sunlight, temperature, water, air, soil, and minerals.

2. The Effects of Human Activities

Human activities, particularly in recent centuries, have greatly changed ecosystems. Significant ways in which humans influence the environment consist of: 

  • Deforestation: The removal of trees for farming, development, and logging diminishes biodiversity, disrupts ecosystems, and raises carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere. 
  • Pollution: Air, water, and soil contamination (e.g., from industrial waste, plastics, and chemicals) damages both living and non-living elements. For instance, harmful substances can destroy marine organisms, and air contamination can impact plant vitality. 
  • Climate Change: Activities caused by humans, especially the combustion of fossil fuels, have resulted in an increase in greenhouse gases, which contributes to global warming. This impacts temperature trends, rainfall, and ecosystems globally, leading to disturbances such as changed migration routes, melting glaciers, and habitat destruction. 
  • Overfishing and Habitat Loss: Excessive use of resources such as fish and wildlife may result in the extinction of species and a decrease in biodiversity. Moreover, actions such as mining, farming, and urban development obliterate habitats. 

Nevertheless, human behaviors can also lead to beneficial effects: 

  • Conservation Initiatives: Individuals have created protected zones, wildlife sanctuaries, and conservation initiatives to safeguard endangered species and rehabilitate ecosystems. 
  • Sustainable Approaches: Initiatives to implement sustainable farming, clean energy, and minimize waste help diminish harmful effects on ecosystems.

3. Biodiversity and Our Duty to Protect It: 

Biodiversity denotes the range of life on Earth, including genetic variation, species variety, and ecosystem diversity. Biodiversity is crucial for the stability and resilience of ecosystems, as well as for providing services that humans depend on, including clean air, water, food, and medicine. 

Maintaining biodiversity is essential for the following reasons: 

  • Ecosystem Services: Robust ecosystems deliver essential services, including pollination, soil enrichment, water cleansing, climate control, and food cultivation. 
  • Moral Obligation: Being the leading species on Earth, humanity has a duty to safeguard the planet’s diverse life forms. The disappearance of species caused by human activities is permanent and has significant effects. 
  • Cultural and Economic Significance: Numerous cultures rely on biodiversity for their traditional customs, spiritual beliefs, and sustenance.

Furthermore, biodiversity holds significant economic worth, spanning agriculture, tourism, and pharmaceuticals. 
Steps to protect biodiversity involve: 

  • Conservation Initiatives: Creating and implementing safeguarded zones such as national parks, wildlife reserves, and marine protected areas. 
  • Sustainable Resource Utilization: Adopting methods that harmonize human requirements with the well-being of ecosystems, including sustainable practices in forestry, fishing, and agriculture. 
  • Reforestation and Rehabilitation: Planting trees and reviving damaged ecosystems can aid in rebuilding biodiversity and alleviating climate change. 

International collaboration is essential for maintaining biodiversity. Initiatives such as the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and other global agreements seek to safeguard species and ecosystems worldwide. 

Transversal Material: 

CLIL (Content and Language Integrated Learning) is a pedagogical method that instructs academic subjects like science, history, or mathematics using a foreign language—specifically, English. By combining language acquisition and content education, students gain both subject expertise and language skills. 


English Vocabulary for Science: In natural science, acquiring specific vocabulary in English aids students in grasping intricate ideas such as "ecosystem," "biodiversity," "photosynthesis," "carbon cycle," and "pollution." This enhances students' capacity to converse on scientific subjects within a global framework and provides access to international studies and publications. 

Scientific Vocabulary and Communication: Mastery of English enables students to express their comprehension of natural science, participate in discussions, compose reports, and read scholarly articles within the international scientific community. 

  • Cognitive Abilities: CLIL fosters cognitive advantages by improving problem-solving, analytical thinking, and critical analysis skills. When students interact with material in a second language, they gain a more profound comprehension of both the language and the content.

Inclusive teaching strategies

Universal Design for Learning (UDL): UDL offers flexible approaches to teaching. In our sessions, we offer: 

  • Multiple Means of Representation: Present content in diverse ways (visual, auditory, kinesthetic).
  • Multiple Means of Action and Expression: It allows students to express understanding in their preferred way.
  • Multiple Means of Engagement: Use activities that tap into students' interests and provide appropriate challenges.

Scaffolding: our activities have appropriate scaffolded points to help the students complete the tasks and also, we guide them through the whole process. We break complex tasks into smaller steps and provide tools (e.g., visual aids, and graphic organizers) to guide students gradually toward independence.


Cooperative learning: 

Encourage group work where students collaborate to achieve shared goals. They work together to accomplish each mission. Each member of the group will provide different skills and knowledge, mixing all kinds of abilities and being points of learning. It also promotes peer support and social skills building.

Methodology

  • Gamification: students have to engage in missions. These missions are active problem-solving. 
  • Challenged-based learning: an instructional approach that encourages students to learn through solving real-world problems or mostly in our case, challenges. 
  • Collaborative learning: there are a lot of activities where they have to work in groups, building social and civic skills.
